Not Forgotten

"History remembers kings and generals,
but Vietnam was carried on the shoulders of women."

For centuries, the history of Vietnam has been told through battles and borders, victories and losses — yet rarely through the women who sustained it all. They fought beside men, led armies, nursed the wounded, and carried messages through jungles. Many never returned home. Their courage was not written in marble statues or schoolbooks, but whispered through generations of mothers and daughters.

This project reimagines their presence not as background figures, but as the heartbeat of the nation's endurance. To look at Vietnam's past through the eyes of its women is to see a different kind of heroism — not the triumph of power, but of persistence. These women resisted not only foreign armies, but also the quiet erasure of their names from history.

The Spirit of Rebellion

Hai Bà Trưng & Lady Triệu

"

"I want to ride the storm, and slay the whale in the sea."

— Lady Triệu, 248 A.D.

Long before the word feminism existed, Vietnam's first revolution was led by women.

In 40 A.D., Trưng Trắc and Trưng Nhị — two sisters from Mê Linh — rose against the Han dynasty, commanding thousands to reclaim independence. Riding elephants draped in silk banners, they turned grief into rebellion after the execution of Trưng Trắc's husband.

Their uprising lasted only three years, but it lit a torch that would never go out. Through five stories across centuries, you will meet the women who rebelled, resisted, and rebuilt — each carrying the same spirit of defiance and care.

Centuries later, Lady Triệu of Thanh Hóa rejected royal submission, vowing to fight rather than "live as a slave to men." She led her troops wearing yellow robes and rode into battle on an elephant, her courage echoing across centuries.

These women were not simply warriors — they were the first to prove that leadership in Vietnam could wear the face of a woman. In every uprising that followed, their names became both war cry and prayer, their courage folded into the soul of the nation. Their legacy was not victory, but the permission to rise.

The Trưng Sisters

In 40 A.D., Trưng Trắc and Trưng Nhị led the first major Vietnamese uprising against Chinese domination. After the execution of Trưng Trắc's husband Thi Sách, the sisters gathered forces and led a rebellion that briefly established an independent Vietnamese state.

The Trưng sisters mobilized an army of 80,000 fighters, both men and women, and successfully liberated 65 citadels. They rode elephants into battle, draped in silk banners, turning their personal grief into a national revolution. Though their rebellion was eventually crushed by the Han general Ma Yuan in 43 A.D., they became eternal symbols of resistance and independence.

Lady Triệu (Triệu Thị Trinh)

Born in 225 A.D. in Thanh Hóa, Lady Triệu (Triệu Thị Trinh) became a legendary warrior who led a rebellion against Chinese rule in 248 A.D. At just 23 years old, she rejected the traditional role of a woman and vowed to fight rather than "live as a slave to men."

She led her troops wearing yellow robes, riding into battle on an elephant, her courage inspiring thousands. Her famous declaration — "I want to ride the storm, and slay the whale in the sea" — became a symbol of Vietnamese resistance. Though her rebellion was eventually crushed, her words survived longer than any empire, echoing through centuries as a testament to the indomitable spirit of Vietnamese women.

Faces of Defiance

Võ Thị Sáu & Nguyễn Thị Định

"I have only one life — I give it to my people."

— Võ Thị Sáu, 1952

At seventeen, Võ Thị Sáu faced her executioners without a blindfold. The colonial soldiers offered her one last chance to plead for life — she smiled instead.

The shots that ended her life could not silence her name; they echoed into every village, every generation that learned courage from a child. To this day, flowers are still placed at her grave in Côn Đảo — not as ritual, but as remembrance.

Years later, Nguyễn Thị Định — known as the "Long-Haired General" — led thousands of unarmed women in the Đồng Khởi movement. They confronted armed soldiers with songs, flags, and bare hands, proving that resistance could be both fierce and tender.

Both women stood against systems that sought to define them by fragility. They turned compassion into strength, endurance into strategy. To fight, for them, was not an act of anger but of care — care for the children who might live if they did not.

Beyond the Battlefield

The Unsung Sisterhood

"They built a nation between bombings —
one bridge, one meal, one letter at a time."

Not every heroine held a weapon. In the shadows of history were the women who cooked, carried, mended, and prayed — the silent backbone of every front line.

The youth volunteers of Trường Sơn carried ammunition through monsoon storms, patched uniforms by candlelight, and buried their friends beside unfinished roads. They were nurses, builders, and teachers all at once.

One photograph shows them laughing beside a broken truck, hair covered in dust. Their joy was an act of resistance; their endurance, a kind of victory. When bombs fell, they sang to steady their hands. When bridges collapsed, they built again.

These women may never have seen medals or monuments, yet every road that crosses Vietnam today bears their fingerprints. Theirs was a quiet revolution, fought with rice, cloth, and love.

Her Strength Today

Legacy and Reflection

"Courage did not end with the war. It changed its form."

Today

Their legacy lives on — not in battlefields, but in classrooms, hospitals, and community halls. The spirit of the heroines continues through every Vietnamese woman who leads, teaches, heals, and dares to speak.

Everywhere

In Hanoi, a journalist challenges gender stereotypes on national television. In Đà Nẵng, a teacher rebuilds a rural school destroyed by floods. In Melbourne, a Vietnamese-Australian scientist leads a team developing renewable energy.

The Legacy

To remember them is to recognise ourselves. When we tell their stories, we are not looking backward but forward — reminding ourselves that the same fire still burns quietly inside us. The women of Vietnam remind us that history is not a book of wars, but a record of compassion. Their legacy is alive not in statues, but in every woman who refuses to give up — who holds her ground with kindness, and turns care into courage.
